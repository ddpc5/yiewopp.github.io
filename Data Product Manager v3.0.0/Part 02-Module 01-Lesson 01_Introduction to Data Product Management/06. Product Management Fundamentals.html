Product Management Fundamentals Summary

As a product manager, you’re constantly:

  • talking to users to empathize with their pain
  • leveraging data to understand macro-trends (market) and micro-trends (experiments)
  • creating solutions with engineers and designers
  • prioritizing the features and bugs in your backlog
  • building a roadmap to have a central point-of-truth on what we’re building next
  • writing specs or PRDs (product requirements documents) to translate your vision into reality
  • creating product marketing materials or sales enablement, so that your product gets to the right users via the most efficient channels

Product managers generally are not primarily focused on:

  • Project management, or driving the development of a feature or project on a tactical and operational level, to ensure that it’s developed on budget, and on-time. This differs based on company size and culture. Generally, for startups and small enterprise companies, there are no dedicated project managers, and product managers have to carry this load. However, for larger companies, there may be project managers on staff, so that product managers could focus more on product strategy and what is called out above.
  • Engineering management, or directly managing engineering reports, reviewing their code, and providing technical mentorship
  • Being the primary decision maker on technical architecture. That’s the primary role of software architects. Great product managers have a good grasp and understanding of the software architecture for the products they own, and have influence over the decision, but have trust that their engineering teams will deliver.
  • Writing code. Half of the product managers I’ve worked with came from engineering backgrounds and still poke at code time-to-time, half came from a non-technical background. Both are equally as successful, assuming that the latter has a good understanding of the capabilities & limitations of their team’s tech stack & microservices.

Product Management Fundamentals Quiz

Out of the following example tasks, what would product managers not be generally responsible for?
SOLUTION:
  • Code reviews and providing technical mentorship to engineers
  • Project management, of the feature to ensure it is delivered on-time and on-budget
