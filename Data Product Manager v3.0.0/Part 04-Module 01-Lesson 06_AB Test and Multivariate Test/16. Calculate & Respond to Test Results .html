Calculate & Respond to Test Results

ND030 C3 L5 11 Calculating & Responding To Test Results Video

Summary

The results experiments can be evaluated by a T-Test. A T-test compares the means of two groups to see whether the difference in the means is large enough that it is probably not due to random chance.

In the case of an A/B or multivariate test, the T-test helps us understand whether any differences observed in the conversion rate are significant and are likely due to the change we made.

To properly evaluate the results, there are 3 steps that need to be taken before using the T-test.

  • Step 1: state the null hypothesis.
    The null hypothesis represents the idea that the control state is not different from a test state, which is what we want to reject.
    A generic null hypothesis would be: customers convert the same with the test state compared to the control state.

  • Step 2: state the alternative hypothesis.
    The alternative hypothesis is the one you want to accept. A generic alternative hypothesis would be: customers will convert differently with this change.

  • Step 3: set a confidence threshold. Product managers almost universally user a 95% confidence level, which means a 95% chance that the result appears statistically valid and a 5% chance that the result is an error.

A higher confidence level means you won’t get as many false positives that declare your experiment likely has an effect when it doesn’t. However, a higher confidence level also means you may declare a test to have not had an effect when it actually does have a weak one. Setting a confidence interval before performing a test ensures that you are not tempted later to lower your confidence threshold to make your results statistically significant.

Once the hypothesis and confidence threshold for an experiment have been set, you can move to:

  • Step 4: run the A/B or multivariate experiment.
  • Step 5: perform a T-test. After you get the result from your experiment, you need to perform a T-test to see if the differences you see in the experiment are significant. To perform a T-test, you will need to know:
    • The number of users in the control group and in each test group
    • The number of conversions in each group
    • The confidence threshold from step 3
    • Decide if the T-test is a one-tailed or two-tailed test. A two-tailed test means you assume the experiment conversion rate is different from the control, maybe worse OR better.

To see whether the results are significant, you need to compare if the p-value from the T-test is less than a threshold. Without going into too many details, if the confidence threshold you choose is 95%, and it is a two-tailed test, you need to compare the p-value against 0.025. When the p-value is less than 0.025, the null hypothesis of experiment and control have the same conversion rate can be rejected. In other words, the difference in the control and experiment conversion rates can be considered significant at 95% confidence.

  • Step 6: take action. If your experiment finds the change to be helpful and the results are statistically significant, you can expand the test condition to more or all of users. If the change doesn’t have the desired effect or the results aren’t significant, you should keep experimenting.

In this example, we see if the results of an A/B test were statistically significant by comparing the control “A” to the experiment “B”. For a multivariate test, you would compare the control “A” to experiments “B” and then “A” to “C” and “A” to “D”and so on to see if any test states have a significant effect.

New terms

  • Alternative hypothesis: Hypothesis in testing that is contrary to the null hypothesis. Usually the alternative hypothesis is that any differences observed are due to a real effect of the experiment.
  • Null hypothesis: Hypothesis in testing that there is no difference between test groups, and that any differences observed are due to error or random chance.
  • P value: The value that indicates the probability of obtaining results as extreme as the observed results of a statistical hypothesis test, assuming that the null hypothesis is correct. A smaller p-value means the evidence in favor of the alternative hypothesis is stronger.
  • T-Test: A type of statistical analysis that compares the means of two groups to see whether the difference in the means is large enough that it is probably not due to random chance.
