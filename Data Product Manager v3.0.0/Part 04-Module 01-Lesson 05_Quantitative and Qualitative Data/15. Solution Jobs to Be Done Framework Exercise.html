15. Solution: Jobs to Be Done Framework Exercise

Jobs to Be Done Framework Exercise Solution

ND030 C3 L4 10 Jobs To Be Done Framework Exercise Solution Video

Summary

1: Definition

To define the job, job-doers must confirm how clean they want their hands to look/feel and how much time they have to spend washing their hands to achieve this result.

2: Location

The job of hand-washing requires locating a sink with running water and some sort of cleaning material (like soap). Optionally, the job might also require locating some way of drying hands.
Note: Remember that the steps in a job map should be generic enough that they apply to all customers, not just how a specific customer does the work. For example, a cleaning product/soap is needed for pre-execution, but if the soap is a bar soap or liquid soap doesn't matter for the job map.

3: Preparation

To prepare the environment for the job of hand-washing, a person might turn on the water to begin to heat it up to the desired temperature. If using a hand towel, they might also place it near the sink to prevent dripping when going from water to hand-drying during the washing process.

4: Confirmation

To ensure they are ready for the job of hand-washing, a person will likely re-check that all the required elements are gathered (water, soap). Hand washers also check their hands to confirm they are ready to be washed and don't have anything on their hands that shouldn't be cleaned, such as jewelry that might slip off or a note written in ink.

5: Execution

When actually carrying out the job, the hand washer must apply soap and water to their hands. They must also move their hands around to get the cleaning supplies over the entire hands. Then, they must run water over their hands until the hands no longer have any soap on them.

6: Monitoring

To ensure they are carrying out the job of hand washing properly, the job doer will watch to make sure soap is actually applied to their hands. They may also look for a physical difference in cleanliness if the dirt on their hands is visible.

7: Modification

While hand washing, a hand washer might need to adjust the pattern with which they move their hands to clean off any visible dirt or soap. For example, especially dirty or sticky hands might require a longer amount of time or a more intense scrub. Fastidious hand washers or those with especially dirty hands/nails might need to introduce an extra scrubbing tool to the process to be successful in removing dirt.

8: Conclusion

At the end of the hand washing job, the hand washer must turn off the water and (optionally) dry off their hands.

Bonus: Emotional Needs Served

When washing hands, a user might serve the emotional need for feeling safer or healthier. They might also be serving the emotional need of showing others that they are healthy people with good habits. Finally, in an environment where hand washing is required (such as a food worker or a doctor at a hospital), there might be an emotional fear of being reprimanded for not hand-washing that motivates the hand washer to perform the act.
