17. Lesson Conclusion

Lesson Conclusion

ND030 C3 L1 17 Lesson Conclusion

Summary

Congratulations! You have just finished the second lesson of this course.

In this lesson, we first discussed the most common business models, including eCommerce, freemium, and software as a service. We learned that revenue-related KPI is a north star for all products and business models, and that leading indicators of revenue production, such as customer satisfaction are also important and should be tracked.

In the second part of this lesson, we discussed the KPIs around the customer journey. We learned about the framework of the Pirate Metrics, which suggests focusing on KPIs related to user acquisition, activation, retention, and revenue. By using all the data we collect to calculate KPIs at each step of the user journey, product managers can discern where they should be spending time and energy to improve a product.

Finally, we learned about instrumenting features and collecting data at a feature-level. The AARR framework can also be leveraged to examine the success of a new feature. We should also track the customer-level data, that is, which customers used the feature, and how and when they used the feature.

Glossary

  • Activation Conversion: When a person goes from being aware of your product to actively using and/or paying for the product.
  • Acquisition: User acquisition metrics measure the number of users who learn about a product or feature.
  • Activation: User activation metrics measure how quickly new visitors decide to become active users and/or paying customers.
  • Activation Conversion: When a person goes from being aware of your product to actively using and/or paying for the product.
  • Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC): How much is spent on acquiring paying customers, per customer acquired.
  • Customer lifetime value (LTV): The amount of revenue an average customer generates.
  • eCommerce: The traditional business model of the sale of an item in exchange for payment. For eCommerce, the storefront is online.
  • HEART Model: Google framework for organizing product and customer data into five different categories: Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, and Task data.
  • KPI: Key Performance Indicator, a metric chosen and watched closely because it provides a good indication of product health/success.
  • Freemium: Business model that allows users to use the basic features of service for free, but charges for upgrades or the removal of advertisements.
  • Leading Indicators: Any metric that changes before the rest of a product's metrics begin to go in a particular direction.
  • **Pirate Metrics **: A set of metrics to help predict whether a product is on the path to success. Created by Doug McLure in 2007, the pirate metric categories are: awareness, acquisition, activation, revenue, retention and referral (AARRR).
  • Referral:: Referral metrics measure how often and how well current users are referring new users to your product.
  • Retention: Retention metrics measure how long, on average, users remain active users and/or paying customers.
  • Revenue:: Revenue metrics measure the revenue (or money earned) by a product or feature.
  • Software as a Service (SAAS): Business model that offers customers access to software in exchange for payment. Typically users have access to the software for as long as they are paying for the service.
