KPIs for Different Business Models Exercise Solution: Choosing KPIs for a Landlord

ND030 C3 L1 07 KPIs For Different Business Models Exercise Solution Video

Question 1: Which business model discussed in this section seems most similar to renting apartments? Why?

When finding similar business models, we must consider the way (and cadence) with which our customers produce revenue. In the case of an apartment complex, users = renters. They pay to get access to an apartment for a limited amount of time. The landlord still retains the valuable asset of the apartment building. In practice, this means the users in the business model of an apartment complex do not own what they purchase. Therefore, an e-commerce model does not fit apartment rentals.

Because users do not get the access to the apartment for free and pay for updates. The landlord business does not reflect the freemium business model.

The best-fit business model is likely SAAS/subscription-based. The renters are "subscribing" to the service of an apartment. The renters do not own the apartment but pay on a regular basis. At the end of their rental period/subscription term, they can choose to move to another apartment or to renew their subscription.

Question 2: What KPIs could you measure as a landlord?

As a landlord, different categories of KPIs can be measured, including the following:

Finding New Tenants (similar to Acquiring New Customers)

  • The time between an apartment is on the market and a lease is signed
  • Cost to find a new tenant (user acquisition cost)

Renter Happiness (similar to Customer Satisfaction)

  • Survey renters about their happiness with their apartment complex and maintenance
  • Renewal rate (the rate at which renters renew their lease)
  • Average number of months renters spend at the complex

Repairs and Maintenance (similar to Customer Support)

  • Number of work orders (similar to the number of customer support tickets)
  • Average time to address work orders (similar to time to address customer support ticket)

Revenues and Costs

  • Net profit: revenue after all costs
  • Revenue per month from rental fees
  • Cost of repairs per month/year
  • Cost of maintenance (new paint, etc.)

Note that this is not an exhaustive list of KPIs for a landlord, but illustrative of the type of KPIs a landlord could measure.

Question 3: If you could only choose 3, which KPIs would you choose to focus on as a landlord? Why?

Revenue is king, whether an online media mogul or an apartment landlord. The top KPIs for a landlord would therefore most likely be profit and revenue related. A landlord must pay the mortgage and taxes on his building, so he must bring in enough revenue from rentals or lose money. Net profit (revenue minus all expenses) is calculable and directly measures the health of the business. Net profit should, therefore, be one of the KPIs you choose to focus on as a landlord.

After profit, the top KPI choices for a landlord focus on those metrics that most reflect the apartment building's capacity to continue to make revenue. Revenue means acquiring or retaining renters, as every empty apartment is an opportunity loss. Given that it is less expensive to keep a renter than to have to acquire a new customer, retention rate (or conversely, churn rate) is likely a KPI that should be in the top 3 of your focus as a landlord.

Additional KPIs you might choose to focus on will likely measure the customer fit/appeal of your apartment building. For example, the time it takes to fill an apartment and/or the satisfaction rating of current tenants. Both of these metrics would be strong indicators of your ability to continue to fill apartments. If you see long gaps before an apartment can be filled, this might indicate the apartment complex/amenities/cost is not a good fit for the market. If satisfaction ratings of current tenants decrease, this may be a leading indicator that leases will not be renewed.
