Summary

This course has 6 lessons.

  • Welcome lesson: you’ll learn about iterative design, its history, and why it is so important to data product managers.
  • Choose & Measure KPIs for Live Products: you’ll learn about key performance indicators for different business models, how to calculate common KPIs and how to instrument and collect data needed to calculate KPIs
  • Evaluate User Conversion Funnels: you'll learn about user conversion funnels, including how to measure and visualize user drop off between steps. By analyzing user funnels, you’ll learn how to reveal potential opportunities for product improvement.
  • Apply User Segment Analysis: you will learn about segment analysis, including how to divide users into cohorts or segments that share similar characteristics and how to apply cohorts and segments to funnel and feature usage analysis. By looking at product data broken into user segments, you’ll be able to identify opportunities to engage and improve the user experience.
  • Quantitative & Qualitative Data: you will dive into qualitative and quantitative data, including the benefits and drawbacks, methods for collecting qualitative data, and ways to integrate data into the iterative design process.
  • A/B & Multivariate Tests: you will learn how to choose an appropriate test for different situations, how to track data during a test, and how to evaluate a test’s results for statistical significance.

Course objective

At the end of the course, you will be able to:

  • Determine key metrics of success for a product based on the product’s business model and place in the development lifecycle.
  • Analyze funnel metrics, feature usage, and other product data to determine product areas in need of optimization.
  • Perform data analysis of KPIs against different user segments/behavioral dimensions to determine user groups to target when designing features
  • Conduct and analyze qualitative user research data to gain insight into the drivers behind user behavior in order to surface new product opportunities.
  • Validate features when engineering resources are not available using qualitative research methods such as prototyping.
  • Design and launch an A/B and multivariate experiment.
  • Evaluate A/B and multivariate experiment results using statistical methods to suggest future areas of research.
