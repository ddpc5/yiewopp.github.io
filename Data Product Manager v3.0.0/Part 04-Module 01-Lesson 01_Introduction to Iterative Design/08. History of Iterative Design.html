History of Iterative Design

Summary

History of iterative design

  • The 1930s: Bell Labs implemented plan-do-study-act cycles of quality improvement.
  • The 1980s: Barry Boehm introduced the Spiral Development Model.
  • In the early 1990s: most software was developed in a “waterfall” style. Product managers would completely plan out all details of a feature and document the specific feature needs.
  • In the late 1990s: Agile methods of design in the software industry began to develop.
  • 1993: Jeff Sutherland ran “Scrum” teams. Scrum emphasizes short periods of development followed by user feedback.
  • 2001: the Agile Manifesto published. The manifesto declared that better products resulted from, among other things, a focus on customer collaboration and a culture of product design that embraces change.
  • 2005: Stanford’s school of design began teaching “design thinking”
  • 2016: Harvard Business Review reported that the iterative design and the use of Agile processes have begun to expand into other business domains

Spiral development model

The spiral development model is a model of developing software. It emphasizes the importance of designing and launching small prototypes before developing and testing a final version of the software. The model uses a spiral instead of a cycle to emphasize that the level of commitment to any new design should begin small and grow over time as ideas are validated with data.

To begin, a software developer needs to determine the objectives for the upcoming project. Then the developer identifies and resolves risks in each small change. Different rings in this example represent different prototypes. Notice that the actual development including coding and testing doesn’t take place until the third time around the spiral development model. Every pass through the spiral ends with planning for the next iteration, which then returns to the beginning of the cycle where objective identification and further building continues.

Waterfall Development

In the waterfall style of development, one team would completely plan out all details of a feature and document the specific feature needs before the next team can act. As a result, the waterfall process takes a long time to build a product, and because no data was sought during creation, finished products and features often failed to appeal to customers.

Agile Development Processes

In the 1990s, software developers developed a new method of Agile development. In contrast to the waterfall, this new method emphasizes short periods of development followed by user feedback. Features and products are iteratively designed in close connection with customers and potential users so that a finished product evolves naturally out of continuous collaboration.

In 2001, thought leaders on this new way of working published the Agile Manifesto. Agile development processes, including Scrum and design thinking, are now widely used across the software industry.
